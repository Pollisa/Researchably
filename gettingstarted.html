Getting Started

When you are beginning a research project, it is often difficult to choose a research topic and find external help. It is always best to pick general fields of interest and narrow down your scope using literature, feasibility, magnitude of problem, and personal interest. This article will help you develop a strong research question to lay a strong foundation for research design and implementation.

1. Picking a Research Topic

First, identify topics or research areas you’re interested in. To do this, think back to the classes you’ve taken and concepts or ideas you’ve found interesting. Consider starting with a couple research questions that focus on a particular aspect of your topic. Your research questions should be based on sources of information that are reliable and can be analyzed.

For example, if you’re interested in bacteriophages, examples of research questions are, “Can phages be used to treat tonsillitis?” or “What mechanisms do phages use to infect bacteria?” Remember to keep an open mind and approach your research question with curiosity.

The problem might be a practical issue—for example, a process or practice that isn’t working well, an area of concern in an organization’s performance, or a difficulty faced by a specific group of people in society.

It is preferred that your research questions address practice problems, such as inefficiencies in a particular technical process, or difficulties faced by particular groups of people. Or, you can dig into theoretical problems, such as new or unexplored phenomena or areas of contention among academics.

2. Reviewing Literature

An important part of getting started is reviewing research papers and reviews. This will help you narrow your research question and determine state-of-the-art methods, current trends and challenges, and areas that need more research. As you read, you should take notes and try to identify problems, lingering questions, areas of debate, and theoretical contradictions. This will help you narrow down your area of interest, and assess how rigorous and feasible your research question is to you.

Sometimes, a research question may be interesting, but is too narrow or too broad. Reviewing literature early on will help you assess the breadth of your topic, allowing you to adjust your goals as needed.

3. Seeking External Opinions

Perhaps the most helpfuls step of starting out is to seek external opinions from experts in your field of interest. Research professors have the best grip on how difficult particular topics are, and can give you advice on research design.

One way to seek professorial support is to find websites for research universities in your field of interest and email the professors at the university. Remember to be clear, concise, and polite. Demonstrate interest in their work, explain your research question, and tell them why their help is important to you.
